Module 03

Content Strategy Framework

Audience-first content strategy — from context nodes and buyer question mapping to persona deep-dives, topic mapping, and the living artifacts that keep strategy honest.

~30 min
4 video clips

What's the most common mistake in content strategy? Starting with keywords. "What has the highest volume? What can we rank for? What's trending?" Wrong questions — all of them. The right question is deceptively simple: "Who is the human we're serving, and what would genuinely help them?"

This is where GrowthX starts. Keywords are useful — they show you what language people use and where demand lives. But they're a tactical input, not a strategy. Strategy starts with understanding who you're writing for at a depth most companies never bother with. I've watched teams spend weeks building keyword maps and content calendars without ever asking what their reader's Tuesday morning actually looks like. That's building a house without checking who's going to live in it.

Clip 01 Context Is Everything — 8.5 min
25 context nodes across 4 categories, LLM context window limitations, buyer question mapping, portfolio strategy.

Real persona work goes way past the one-page template you've probably seen. You need to know their daily reality — what does this person's actual workday feel like? Their career trajectory — where are they headed, and what's in the way? Their jobs-to-be-done — not your product's jobs, theirs. The deliverables their boss judges them on. What gets them promoted, what gets them fired. What makes them lean into a piece of content, and what makes them bounce. What event or pain threshold finally tips them from browsing into evaluating. Get any of those wrong and you're writing for a persona that doesn't exist.

Clip 02 Audience-First Deep Dive — 5 min
Job description, daily reality, JTBD, deliverables, KPIs, promotion/firing triggers, emotional mapping.

Here's why this matters even more in the AI era. Content is becoming long-tail and personal. Nobody Googled "I'm a finance manager at a 30-person Series A company in healthcare trying to reduce time spent sorting transactions." But people absolutely type that into ChatGPT. Full context. Full situation. Expecting a personalized answer. If your content speaks to that specific reality — because you deeply understand that person — you become the source the AI reaches for. Generic content written for "finance managers" broadly? That's noise. Specific content written for the actual human? That's signal.

What do AI engines reward when they're deciding what to cite? Four things. Content that's well-researched and well-attributed — sources, data, named experts. Content that's comprehensive — covers the topic thoroughly enough that the AI doesn't need to stitch together multiple sources. Content from brand-reputable domains — sites with authority signals, consistent publishing, real expertise. And content that's fresh — recently published or updated. None of these are SEO tactics. They're qualities of content that's actually useful to a real person. The AI engines didn't invent new criteria — they just got better at measuring the ones that always mattered.

Clip 03 Content Strategy Walkthrough — 8 min
Research/audit → discovery → artifacts (company context, audience personas, writing guidelines).

All of this gets encoded into what we call artifacts — living documents that make your strategy usable. A company context artifact captures your positioning, differentiators, proof points. Audience persona artifacts are built from the deep research above. Writing guidelines define voice, tone, style rules, with good and bad examples. If you're a developer, think of these like cursor rules or claude.md files — they evolve with every use, and they compound. The first article you produce with full artifacts takes longer. By the fiftieth, you barely have to think about positioning or voice because the context is already there, pressure-tested across dozens of pieces.

Clip 04 Topic Mapping — 5 min
Three angles: competitive mind-share, audience JTBD, solution enablement. Knowledge graph per persona.

Know your audience at a depth that would surprise them. Everything else follows from that.

Key Takeaways

  • Start with your audience, not keywords. Understanding daily reality, pain points, and jobs-to-be-done comes first.
  • LLMs reward four things: well-researched/attributed, comprehensive, brand-reputable, and fresh content.
  • Content is becoming "long-tail and personal" — buyers now type detailed, contextual queries into AI.
  • Artifacts (company context, audience personas, writing guidelines) are living documents — your team's shared source of truth that compounds over time.
  • Content that's genuinely useful to a human reader is what AI models prefer to cite. The criteria aren't new.
